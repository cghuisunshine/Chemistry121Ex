Chemistry • Bonding Models

Valence Bond Theory (VBT) vs Molecular Orbital Theory (MOT)

Two quantum “pictures” of the same underlying physics: VBT emphasizes localized bonds and shape; MOT emphasizes delocalized orbitals, electron occupancy, and properties like magnetism and bond order.

A Big Picture & Origin
▶

What each theory is

Definition Valence Bond Theory (VBT)

Textbook definition: A chemical bond forms when two atoms contribute orbitals that overlap and two electrons occupy the resulting bonding region with opposite spins (a localized electron-pair bond).

Plain language: Think “bonds are between specific atoms,” like sticks connecting balls.

Logical meaning: Use localized orbitals (often hybrids) to match observed geometry and bond directionality.

Conditions: Best when a localized bonding picture is appropriate; delocalization is handled indirectly via resonance.

Definition Molecular Orbital Theory (MOT)

Textbook definition: Atomic orbitals combine (LCAO) to form molecular orbitals that extend over the entire molecule; electrons occupy these MOs following Aufbau, Pauli, and Hund rules.

Plain language: Electrons can belong to the whole molecule, not just one bond.

Physical meaning: Electron density and nodes in MOs explain stability, spectroscopy, and magnetism.

Conditions: Especially powerful for diatomics, conjugation, aromaticity, and magnetic properties.

Why they were introduced (historical motivation)

  • Lewis structures (electron pairs) explained valence but did not provide a quantum-mechanical wavefunction or predict magnetic behavior reliably.
  • VBT (Heitler–London, 1927) explained why H2 is stable: overlap + electron exchange lowers energy and favors paired spins in a bonding region.
  • MOT (Hund, Mulliken) became essential when delocalization and spectroscopy mattered—most famously, it naturally explains why O2 is paramagnetic.

What problems each model solves best

Quick Map “Use this when…”
  • VBT shines: geometry (hybridization), directed bonds, sigma/pi bonding language, resonance as a structure tool.
  • MOT shines: bond order trends, magnetism, delocalization (one MO description instead of many resonance structures), qualitative spectra/excited states.
B Core Concepts & Definitions
▶

VBT core tools

Definition Overlap (VBT)

Textbook definition: The spatial overlap of two atomic (or hybrid) orbitals, associated with increased electron density between nuclei in a bonding interaction.

Plain language: More “orbital sharing” in the internuclear region usually means a stronger bond (within a simple model).

Assumptions: Works best as a qualitative guide; true bond strength depends on full electronic structure, repulsions, and correlation.

Definition Hybridization (VBT)

Textbook definition: Linear combination of an atom’s valence orbitals (e.g., s and p) to form equivalent hybrid orbitals oriented for bonding (sp, sp2, sp3, …).

Logical meaning: A bookkeeping/geometry model: it explains observed bond angles and “directional” bonding.

Validity: Most useful in organic/main-group structure; not a unique physical observable (it’s a chosen orbital basis).

Definition Resonance (VBT)

Textbook definition: A molecule is described as a superposition of multiple contributing Lewis/VB structures when no single structure captures electron distribution.

Plain language: You draw several reasonable pictures; the real molecule is “in between.”

Common use: benzene, allyl systems, carbonate, nitrite, etc.

MOT core tools

Definition Molecular Orbital (MO)

Textbook definition: A one-electron wavefunction for a molecule; electrons occupy MOs across the whole molecule.

Physical meaning: Nodes/phase and spatial extent control bonding, antibonding, and electron density distribution.

Units: Orbitals are wavefunctions; energies are often reported in eV or kJ·mol−1 depending on context.

Definition Bonding vs Antibonding MOs

Bonding: in-phase combination increases electron density between nuclei → stabilizes.

Antibonding: out-of-phase combination introduces a node between nuclei → destabilizes (marked with *, e.g., σ*, π*).

Definition Bond Order (MOT)

Textbook definition: A measure of net bonding from MO occupancy.

Bond order (BO) = (N_bonding − N_antibonding) / 2

Interpretation: Larger BO → typically shorter, stronger bonds (trend-level rule).

Definition Magnetism criterion

Paramagnetic: ≥1 unpaired electron (attracted to a magnetic field).

Diamagnetic: all electrons paired (weakly repelled).

In exams: MO diagrams make this almost automatic once you fill correctly.

Assumptions & limitations (what to trust)

  • Both theories are models derived from quantum mechanics; they differ mainly by the orbital basis (localized vs delocalized) and the approximations emphasized.
  • VBT limitation: struggles to predict magnetism and delocalization without extra constructs (resonance, configuration mixing).
  • MOT limitation: can feel less intuitive for “where a bond is”; diagrams can be misapplied if ordering is wrong or if the molecule isn’t well-approximated as simple diatomic MO filling.
C Equations, Laws, or Theorems
▶
Equation LCAO combinations (qualitative)

Statement: combining two similar atomic orbitals φA, φB yields two MOs: one bonding and one antibonding.

ψ± = c(φA ± φB)

Symbols: φA, φB are atomic orbitals; c is a normalization/mixing constant; + gives bonding, − gives antibonding (phase matters).

Applies when: orbitals have appropriate symmetry and comparable energies; overlap is non-negligible.

Equation Bond order from MO occupancy
BO = (N_bonding − N_antibonding) / 2

Meaning: counts net bonding after antibonding electrons “cancel” bonding electrons.

Applies best to: simple MO pictures (especially diatomics). Still useful as a trend tool more broadly.

Equation Overlap integral (qualitative bonding strength guide)
S = ∫ φA(r) φB(r) dτ

Symbols: S overlap integral; dτ volume element; r electron coordinate.

Interpretation: larger positive overlap generally supports stronger bonding in simple VB thinking, but not a complete predictor of bond energy.

D Derivations or Proofs
▶
Derivation Why bonding vs antibonding MOs appear
  1. Assumption: start with two AOs of compatible symmetry, φA and φB.
  2. Linear combination: the simplest two independent combinations are φA+φB and φA−φB.
  3. Physical meaning of “+”: in-phase addition increases electron density between nuclei → lowers potential energy (electron-nucleus attraction wins) → bonding.
  4. Physical meaning of “−”: out-of-phase subtraction forces a node between nuclei → less electron density between nuclei → higher energy → antibonding.
  5. Why two? Because the space spanned by {φA, φB} is 2D; you get two orthogonal combinations.
Derivation Bond order formula (reasoning)
  1. Assumption: a filled bonding MO (2 electrons paired) corresponds roughly to one bond’s worth of stabilizing density between nuclei.
  2. So each electron in a bonding MO contributes about +½ to bond order; each electron in an antibonding MO contributes about −½.
  3. Summing contributions gives 
    BO = (N_bonding − N_antibonding) / 2
  4. Interpretation: antibonding occupancy partially cancels bonding occupancy; BO predicts trends in bond length/strength.
Derivation Why O2 is paramagnetic (MO filling logic)
  1. Assumption: use the standard MO set for O2 (for O2/F2, the usual ordering has σ2p below π2p).
  2. Count valence electrons: O has 6 valence e−, so O2 has 12 valence e−.
  3. Fill MOs: after filling bonding MOs, the last electrons enter the degenerate π* antibonding orbitals.
  4. Hund’s rule: degenerate orbitals fill singly before pairing → leaves two unpaired electrons.
  5. Conclusion: O2 is paramagnetic (matches experiment).
E Worked Examples
▶
Example 1 H2: VBT and MOT agree (simple covalent bond)

Goal: decide stability, bond order, and magnetism.

  • VBT idea: 1s orbitals overlap end-on → σ bond; two electrons pair in bonding region.
  • MOT electron count: 2 valence e− total.
  • Fill: σ1s gets 2 e−, σ*1s gets 0.
BO = (2 − 0)/2 = 1  → stable single bond; all electrons paired → diamagnetic
Common Trap Thinking “MO is always complicated”

For many cases, MO is just systematic electron counting + filling—H2 is the cleanest example.

Example 2 He2: why it doesn’t form a stable molecule (MO explains fast)

Count valence electrons: 4 total (2 from each He).

Fill: σ1s has 2 e−, σ*1s has 2 e−.

BO = (2 − 2)/2 = 0  → no net bond (not stable as a molecule)

Intuition: antibonding electrons cancel bonding stabilization completely.

Example 3 B2 vs N2: bond order and magnetism trends (ordering matters)

Key idea: For lighter homonuclear diatomics (roughly B2–N2), s–p mixing can place π2p below σ2p. (Many courses test this as a known rule.)

  • B2 (6 valence e−): after σ2s and σ*2s, the next electrons enter π2p (degenerate) → two unpaired electrons → paramagnetic.
  • N2 (10 valence e−): fills bonding 2p MOs fully before antibonding → BO = 3, diamagnetic.
Common Trap Using the wrong MO ordering

If you use the O2-type ordering for B2/C2/N2, you’ll get wrong magnetism predictions—an easy exam point loss.

Example 4 O2 and F2: same framework, different outcomes

O2 (12 valence e−): ends with two unpaired electrons in π* → paramagnetic, and

O2: BO = 2

F2 (14 valence e−): adds two more electrons that pair up in π* → diamagnetic, and

F2: BO = 1

Trend: lower BO → longer/weaker bond (F–F is notably weak among halogens due to additional effects too, but MO BO gets the direction right).

Example 5 NO vs NO+: predicting bond strength changes by electron count

Strategy: treat as “like O2 but with one less/more electron” in many introductory MO treatments.

  • NO has 11 valence e− → one unpaired electron → typically paramagnetic.
  • NO+ has 10 valence e− → tends to increase bond order relative to NO → shorter/stronger bond.
Common Trap Mixing “formal charge” rules with MO filling

In MO problems, the cleanest path is almost always: count valence electrons → fill → BO + magnetism. Formal charge is a different bookkeeping tool.

F Common Questions & Misconceptions
▶
Common Trap “One of these is correct; the other is wrong.”

Both come from quantum mechanics. They are different representations (localized vs delocalized) with different approximations and strengths.

Common Trap “Bond order = number of lines in the Lewis structure, always.”

Lewis/VBT lines are a localized picture; MO bond order is an occupancy-based measure. They often match for simple molecules, but diverge with radicals, ions, and delocalized systems.

Common Trap “MO ordering is universal.”

Many curricula use two orderings: one for B2–N2 (π2p below σ2p) and one for O2–F2 (σ2p below π2p). Know which your course expects.

Q&A Typical student questions
  • Why does MOT predict magnetism so well? Because unpaired electrons are explicit in the occupancy pattern.
  • Does resonance mean the molecule flips between structures? No—resonance structures are not real, isolated states; the real electron distribution is a single quantum state better represented as a combination.
  • Can VBT handle delocalization? Yes, but usually through resonance/configuration mixing rather than a single set of delocalized orbitals.
G Exam Strategy (Short & Focused)
▶
Checklist Fast method selection
  • Geometry / bond angles / hybridization / resonance? Start with VBT + Lewis (then refine with resonance/hybridization).
  • Bond order / magnetism / diatomic comparisons? Use MO diagram + BO.
  • “Explain O2 paramagnetism” is a direct MOT trigger.
Checklist MO diagram workflow (diatomics)
  1. Count valence electrons (include charge).
  2. Pick the correct ordering for your course (B–N vs O–F rule, if used).
  3. Fill using Aufbau, Pauli, Hund.
  4. Compute 
    BO = (N_b − N_a)/2
  5. Decide paramagnetic vs diamagnetic from unpaired electrons.
  6. Translate BO to trends: higher BO → shorter/stronger (as a rule of thumb).
Common Trap “Filling” without labeling bonding/antibonding

Always mark which orbitals are bonding vs antibonding before you count Nb and Na.

H Summary & Connections
▶
Summary Plain-language takeaways
  • VBT: localized bonds + hybridization → excellent for structure and “bond directions.”
  • MOT: delocalized orbitals + occupancy → excellent for bond order trends and magnetism (e.g., O2).
  • They are complementary: same quantum mechanics, different emphasis and bookkeeping.
Connections Where this shows up next
  • Conjugation/aromaticity: MO delocalization explains stability and reactivity patterns.
  • Coordination chemistry: ligand field theory and MO descriptions of metal–ligand bonding extend these ideas.
  • Solid-state/band theory: MO ideas generalize to bands in solids.
  • Spectroscopy: electronic transitions are naturally described with MOs.

# VBT vs MOT — Valence Bond Theory vs Molecular Orbital Theory (Chemistry)

## A) Big Picture & Origin
- **What it is:** Two complementary quantum models for chemical bonding.
  - **VBT (Valence Bond Theory):** Bonds form from *localized* overlap of atomic orbitals; electrons are paired in bonds/lone pairs.
  - **MOT (Molecular Orbital Theory):** Atomic orbitals combine to form *delocalized* molecular orbitals occupied by electrons across the whole molecule.
- **Why introduced historically:** Early Lewis structures explained valence, but needed a quantum explanation for bond strength, directionality, and spectroscopy.
  - VBT grew from Heitler–London (H₂) to explain stable covalent bonding via exchange and overlap.
  - MOT developed (Hund–Mulliken) to explain spectra, delocalization, and magnetic properties (notably O₂ paramagnetism).
- **What problems they solve:**
  - VBT: geometry, directed bonds, hybridization, resonance pictures.
  - MOT: bond order trends, magnetism, electron delocalization, excited states qualitatively.
- **What earlier ideas they extend/unify:** They refine Lewis/Kekulé models with wavefunctions and the Pauli principle; both derive from the same quantum mechanics but choose different “pictures” (localized vs delocalized).

## B) Core Concepts & Definitions
- **Valence bond (VB) description:** A bond is an electron pair in an orbital formed by overlap of two atomic (or hybrid) orbitals.
- **Hybridization (VBT tool):** Mixing of s and p orbitals on one atom to create directed hybrids (sp, sp², sp³) matching molecular shapes.
- **Resonance (VBT tool):** When one Lewis structure is insufficient, multiple contributing structures represent a delocalized electron distribution.
- **Molecular orbitals (MO):** Orbitals that belong to the whole molecule; electrons occupy them according to Aufbau, Pauli, Hund.
- **Bonding vs antibonding MOs:** In-phase combination lowers energy (bonding); out-of-phase raises energy with a node (antibonding, marked * or σ*, π*).
- **Bond order (MOT tool):** BO = (N_bonding − N_antibonding)/2.
- **Magnetism:** Paramagnetic species have unpaired electrons; diamagnetic have all electrons paired.
- **Validity/assumptions:** Both are approximations; choice depends on what property you care about and the level of theory.

## C) Equations, Laws, or Theorems
- **LCAO combinations (qualitative):** ψ± = c(φ_A ± φ_B) → bonding/antibonding.
- **Bond order:** BO = (N_b − N_a)/2.
- **Overlap integral (qualitative):** S = ∫ φ_A φ_B dτ; larger overlap usually → stronger bond (in the simplest picture).

## D) Derivations or Proofs
- **Why bonding/antibonding form:** Two AO combinations are orthogonal; in-phase increases electron density between nuclei → lower potential energy; out-of-phase creates a node → reduced density between nuclei → higher energy.
- **Bond order formula reasoning:** Each electron in a bonding MO contributes ~½ to a bond (pair → 1), each in antibonding cancels similarly → BO = (N_b − N_a)/2.
- **O₂ paramagnetism (MO reasoning):** Filling places two unpaired electrons in π* orbitals → predicts paramagnetism.

## E) Worked Examples
1. **H₂:** VBT: σ bond from 1s–1s overlap. MOT: σ1s filled → BO=1, diamagnetic.
2. **He₂:** MOT: σ1s and σ*1s both filled → BO=0 (no stable bond).
3. **O₂:** MOT: two unpaired e⁻ in π* → BO=2, paramagnetic.
4. **N₂:** MOT: BO=3, diamagnetic; explains very strong triple bond.
5. **NO vs NO⁺:** Use electron count; BO changes; predicts bond length/strength trends.

## F) Common Questions & Misconceptions
- “Is one theory correct and the other wrong?” → Both are approximations; same QM, different representations.
- “Bond order always equals number of lines in Lewis?” → Often, but not always (delocalization, radicals, ions).
- “Always use the same MO ordering.” → For lighter diatomics (B₂–N₂) σ2p/π2p ordering differs from O₂–F₂.

## G) Exam Strategy (Short & Focused)
- If asked **geometry/hybridization/resonance** → start with VBT + Lewis.
- If asked **magnetism/bond order/diatomic trends** → MO diagram + BO formula.
- Checklist: count valence e⁻ → choose ordering → fill → compute BO → determine magnetism → compare to bond length/strength.

## H) Summary & Connections
- VBT = localized bonds; great for structure and “where the bonds are.”
- MOT = delocalized orbitals; great for magnetism, spectra, and delocalized bonding.
- Connections: spectroscopy, conjugation/aromaticity, band theory (solids), coordination chemistry.
